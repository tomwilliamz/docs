## "Where do these numbers come from?"

Every input has a traceable source:

| Input | Source |
|-------|--------|
| **Financial pool (M0)** | ARR, pipeline, or budget data from public filings, CRM, or seller discovery |
| **Current metric (S0)** | CRM data, customer-reported metrics, or industry benchmarks |
| **Target metric (S1)** | The sales conversation about what's achievable |
| **Adoption rates** | Implementation experience and industry data, adjustable by the seller |

<Tip>If a customer challenges any input, that's a *good* thing. Invite them to set their own numbers. When they own the assumptions, they trust the output.</Tip>

---

## "Why should I trust your adoption numbers?"

The adoption rates (a0, a1_diy, a1_vendor) are the most subjective inputs, and that's by design. They're explicitly editable, and the customer can adjust them. The model recalculates instantly.

Challenge them to put in their own numbers - the gap between DIY and Vendor adoption almost always holds because of:
- Dedicated support and onboarding
- Purpose-built tooling vs. internal workarounds
- Training and enablement programs
- Accountability and measurement built into the solution

---

## "What if our metrics are different from what you've assumed?"

Change them. The model is fully parameterized. If the customer says their win rate is 25% not 20%, update S0 and the entire business case recalculates.

This is a feature, not a bug - it turns the conversation from *"do you believe our ROI claim?"* to *"let's agree on the inputs and let the math speak."*

---

## "How does this compound over time?"

The model runs quarter-by-quarter over the full time horizon (typically 8-12 quarters).

For most KPIs, the quarterly impact stays flat once adoption reaches steady state.

For **churn and NDR** specifically, the impact **compounds** - each year builds on the larger retained base from the previous year, creating an exponential divergence between Do Nothing and the improvement scenarios.

<Note>This compounding effect is one of the most powerful points for retention-focused products. A 2% churn reduction doesn't just save 2% of ARR - it saves 2% of an ever-growing base.</Note>

---

## "What's the payback period?"

Compare cumulative impact against cumulative costs quarter by quarter. The crossover point is the payback period.

For Vendor scenarios, this typically accounts for:
- Initial license/setup costs
- Implementation fees
- Ongoing subscription
- Offset against the ramping financial impact from improved adoption

---

## "This seems too good to be true."

Point to three things:

1. **The inputs are theirs.** S0, S1, and M0 all trace back to the customer's own data or what they told you in discovery. There's no hidden multiplier.

2. **We show the downside.** The P10-P25 band shows the conservative case. We're not hiding behind best-case numbers.

3. **DIY is on the table.** If we were inflating numbers, we wouldn't show a DIY option that gets partway there. The honesty of the three-option framework is the credibility mechanism.

---

## "Our finance team won't accept this."

The model is designed specifically for finance scrutiny:

- **Confidence ranges** instead of point estimates (how finance teams forecast)
- **Traceable inputs** - every number has a source
- **No hidden multipliers** - the math is transparent
- **Conservative defaults** - adoption rates and uncertainty bands lean conservative
- **Quarter-by-quarter modeling** - not a single annual number, but a time series that shows the ramp

Ask the finance team to set their own assumptions. The model works with whatever inputs they trust.
