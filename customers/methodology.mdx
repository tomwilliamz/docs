## Principles

<Steps>
  <Step title="No magic multipliers">
    We never apply a hidden "our solution delivers X% improvement" factor. The metric improvement target (S1) is set based on your data and conversation, not a vendor claim.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Transparent inputs">
    Every number in the model traces back to a source: your CRM, public financials, or what your team shared. Nothing is hidden.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Three honest options">
    We always show Do Nothing, DIY, and Vendor. We acknowledge that solving it internally is a valid path - the difference is how much of the organization actually adopts the improvement.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Three scenarios, defensible math">
    Every business case is presented as Conservative, Likely, and Stretch - three deterministic recalculations of the same model under different assumption sets. No statistical sampling, no hidden distributions. Each scenario can be defended line by line.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## How the Model Works

### The Core Idea

The financial impact of any improvement depends on three things:

1. **How big is the financial pool?** (M0) - The dollars flowing through the metric you're trying to improve
2. **How much can the metric improve?** (S0 to S1) - The gap between current and achievable performance
3. **How much of the organization will actually adopt the improvement?** (a0 to a1) - This is what differentiates DIY from Vendor

### Adoption Is the Differentiator

The metric target (S1) is the same whether you go DIY or Vendor. What changes is the **adoption ceiling** - the percentage of your organization that actually implements and sustains the new behavior.

Vendor solutions typically achieve higher adoption because of dedicated tooling, training, support, and accountability mechanisms that aren't present in DIY approaches.

### Time-Aware Modeling

Impact doesn't arrive on day one. The model accounts for:

- **Rollout time** - How long before users start adopting
- **Proficiency ramp** - Even adopted users aren't at full productivity immediately
- **Quarter-by-quarter progression** - Impact builds over time as more users adopt and become proficient

This produces a realistic trajectory where Year 1 shows the ramp and Year 2+ shows the steady-state value.

### Three Scenarios

Rather than a single number, the model presents three deterministic scenarios:

- **Conservative** - lower lift, slower ramp, or longer time-to-impact
- **Likely** - the seller's defensible base case (the single value they typed)
- **Stretch** - higher lift, faster ramp, or earlier impact

Conservative and Stretch are derived from Likely using rules set by your organization's RevOps or Finance team. Each rule is transparent: "Conservative = 70% of the Likely lift, set by RevOps for this KPI class." Your finance team can see the exact transformation that produced each scenario number.

This replaces the statistical sampling approach that earlier tools relied on. For the deal sizes Supercase typically models, sampling-based confidence intervals collapse to near-zero anyway - they were giving false precision. Named scenarios with auditable rules match how finance teams actually plan.

---

### Multi-KPI Compounding

When a supercase covers multiple KPIs that compound together - for example, growing pipeline volume *and* improving win rate - the combined impact is **larger than the sum of the individual gains**. The model surfaces this compounding amount explicitly so the customer can see both the per-KPI math and the joint result, without double-counting.
