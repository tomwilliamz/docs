## What the Customer Sees

A side-by-side comparison of three scenarios, all built from the same inputs:

| | Do Nothing | DIY | Vendor |
|---|-----------|-----|--------|
| **Year 1 Impact** | -$X lost | +$Y gained | +$Z gained |
| **Year 2 Run Rate** | -$X/year ongoing | +$Y/year steady state | +$Z/year steady state |
| **Total Impact** | -$XX cumulative | +$YY cumulative | +$ZZ cumulative |
| **Costs** | $0 | Implementation + internal | License + implementation |
| **Net Impact** | -$XX | $YY - costs | $ZZ - costs |
| **Final Adoption** | a0 (unchanged) | a1_diy | a1_vendor |

---

## The Five Talking Points

### 1. "The cost exists whether you buy from us or not."

The Do Nothing column shows money being left on the table regardless of vendor choice. This isn't a scare tactic - it's the math on the gap between where they are and where they could be.

### 2. "DIY gets you partway there."

Acknowledging that DIY works - just not as well - builds trust and avoids the false binary of "buy or fail." The customer knows DIY is an option. Showing it honestly makes the rest of the analysis credible.

### 3. "The difference is adoption, not magic."

The metric target (S1) is the same for DIY and Vendor. The vendor advantage is getting more of the organization to actually adopt and sustain the improvement. This is a defensible claim because it's about change management, not a magic feature.

### 4. "Every number traces back to your data."

S0 came from discovery/CRM. M0 came from financials. S1 is what *you* said was achievable. Nothing is hidden. If any input is wrong, change it and the model recalculates.

### 5. "The ranges are honest."

Conservative to optimistic bands show we're not cherry-picking the best-case number. The P50 (median) is the headline, but the P10 shows what happens even if things don't go perfectly.

## How to Use This in a Meeting

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start with Do Nothing">
    Anchor the conversation on what they're losing today. This isn't hypothetical - it's derived from their own metrics and financials.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Acknowledge DIY">
    Show that you respect their ability to solve this internally. Walk through the DIY column. The adoption ceiling is the honest differentiator.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Show the Vendor Uplift">
    The delta between DIY and Vendor is driven entirely by adoption rate. Ask: "Do you think your team would adopt this at 60% on their own, or 85% with dedicated tooling and support?"
  </Step>
  <Step title="Let Them Challenge the Inputs">
    Invite the customer to adjust any number. When they engage with the inputs, they own the output. A CFO who set the assumptions will trust the conclusion.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Net Impact and Payback

The bottom line for finance teams:

- **Net Impact** = Total cumulative impact minus total costs (license, implementation, internal effort)
- **Payback Period** = The quarter where cumulative impact exceeds cumulative costs
- **Year 2 Run Rate** = The steady-state annual value once adoption has fully ramped

For Vendor scenarios, this typically accounts for initial license costs, implementation fees, and ongoing subscription, offset against the ramping financial impact.
